\chapter{EXPERIMENTAL STUDY}

This chapter documents the current state of the experimental study in two
complementary ways. First, it reports two completed pilot comparisons under a
shared setup: an MMLU routing-versus-standalone comparison and a same-model
GSM8K three-way comparison across single-SLM, hybrid, and single-LLM modes.
Second, it clarifies which parts of the broader thesis evaluation pipeline are
already usable and which still remain preliminary. This separation is important
because the repository can already produce meaningful pilot evidence, but not
every benchmark surface is yet mature enough for thesis-grade claims.

\section{Current MMLU Comparison Setup}

The present study reports two pilot MMLU comparisons to evaluate two
response-generation strategies under identical benchmark conditions. The routing run,
\texttt{exp\_ecdd81b2}, first answers with the lightweight
\texttt{gemma4-4b} model and escalates uncertain cases to
\texttt{gemma4-31b}. The standalone run, \texttt{exp\_018ab0cf}, answers every
question directly with \texttt{gemma4-31b}. Since both runs evaluate the same
\texttt{mmlu} benchmark with 100 samples and zero temperature, the comparison
isolates the effect of architecture choice rather than dataset variation.

The comparison is organised across five evaluation dimensions: accuracy, EATS, latency,
cost, and resource usage. As defined in Section~\ref{sec:eats}, EATS denotes the
Efficiency-Accuracy Trade-off Score and rewards architectures that preserve
accuracy while reducing normalised cost, latency, and energy relative to a
full-LLM standalone baseline. A
higher EATS therefore indicates a more favourable efficiency-accuracy balance.

\section{Accuracy and EATS Analysis}

Figure~\ref{fig:mmlu-scores} compares the two architectures on raw task
accuracy. The standalone design achieved the higher MMLU accuracy with 85
correct answers out of 100, while the routing design reached 76 correct
answers. This 9 percentage-point gap is expected because the standalone
baseline always invokes the stronger \texttt{gemma4-31b} model.

The EATS comparison yields a more nuanced result. Routing reached an
EATS of 0.325, while the standalone baseline reached 0.419. Under the current
accuracy-first formulation, the routing architecture does not achieve a superior EATS score
because its latency penalty outweighs its cost and energy savings.
The primary contributor to this penalty is selective escalation overhead: 66 samples were resolved at
the SLM stage, but 34 required LLM escalation and every routing request still
incurred an initial SLM inference pass.

\section{Efficiency and Resource Comparison}

Figure~\ref{fig:mmlu-efficiency} shows the operating-cost side of the same
comparison. Routing reduced heavy-model usage from 100\% to 34\%, lowered total
energy from 0.0826 to 0.0665 kWh, and reduced estimated CO$_2$ emissions from
31.39 g to 25.28 g. The cost difference was smaller but still favorable to
routing: \$0.5913 versus \$0.6066, or roughly a 2.5\% reduction.

The latency trade-off is much sharper. Average latency increased from 9.29
seconds in the standalone run to 20.45 seconds in routing, and tail latency
(p95) increased from 17.66 seconds to 42.18 seconds. This overhead comes from
the two-stage control flow. Every routing request pays for an initial SLM pass,
and 34 requests also pay for a second LLM pass. Within the routing run, the
66 SLM-only samples averaged 16.26 seconds, while escalated samples averaged
28.57 seconds.

The escalation breakdown also helps explain the result. Out of the 34 escalated
samples, 23 were escalated because confidence fell below the 0.8 threshold and
11 were escalated because the draft answer could not be parsed reliably. In
other words, part of the latency penalty comes from router conservatism and
output-format failures, not only from difficult questions.

\section{Discussion of the Current Result}

These two experiments demonstrate that architecture choice alters the operating point
of the system rather than producing a universal winner. If the primary objective is
maximum MMLU accuracy, the standalone \texttt{gemma4-31b} baseline is stronger.
If the objective is to reduce heavy-model invocation frequency, energy, and carbon while
keeping accuracy within an acceptable range, the routing strategy is more attractive.

At the same time, these runs should still be interpreted as a focused pilot
comparison rather than as the final thesis result set. They demonstrate that the
reporting stack, experiment runner, and cost-energy instrumentation can support
meaningful architecture analysis, but they do not yet replace the broader coding
benchmark that the thesis is designed around.

\section{Same-Model GSM8K Three-Way Comparison}

This report also includes a second pilot comparison that keeps the model family
fixed and changes only the response-generation mode. The three runs all evaluate
\texttt{gsm8k} with 100 samples at zero temperature. The goal is to compare three
operating points within the same Gemma family: a single-SLM baseline, a routing
hybrid, and a standalone LLM baseline. The single-SLM baseline is
\texttt{exp\_efec5356}. Behaviorally, it is a single-model \texttt{gemma4-4b}
baseline, although the implementation path uses the repository's
\texttt{ensemble} architecture with \texttt{n\_models=1}. The hybrid run,
\texttt{exp\_b4a9b415}, answers first with \texttt{gemma4-4b} and escalates to
\texttt{gemma4-31b} when needed. The standalone baseline,
\texttt{exp\_a7d53595}, answers every sample directly with \texttt{gemma4-31b}.

The accuracy ordering is unambiguous. The standalone LLM baseline
produced the highest GSM8K accuracy at 95\%. The routing hybrid reached 91\%,
which is 4 percentage points lower than the standalone LLM baseline but 8 percentage
points higher than the single-SLM baseline at 83\%. The routing system thus
occupies an intermediate operating point: it does not match the best-quality configuration,
but it meaningfully improves answer quality over the lightweight-only
baseline while still limiting heavy-model invocations.

The efficiency results present a more complex picture. Relative to the standalone LLM run, routing
reduced total cost from \$0.3869 to \$0.2888, a reduction of approximately 25.4\%. The
same hybrid run also reduced total energy and estimated CO$_2$ from 0.0527 to
0.0306 kWh and from 20.02 to 11.63 g respectively, corresponding to approximately
41.9\% lower energy use and emissions. The single-SLM baseline remained the
cheapest and least energy-intensive operating point overall, but it trailed the
standalone LLM baseline by 12 percentage points of accuracy and the routing hybrid by 8
points.

The latency results merit more careful interpretation. Routing was approximately 94\% slower on
average than the standalone LLM run, increasing mean latency from 5.93 to 11.51
seconds. It was also approximately 12.4\% slower than the single-SLM baseline. This
does not imply that the smaller model is inherently slower. In this setup, the
standalone LLM benefits from a dedicated host class and a single-stage control
flow, while the routing system incurs an initial SLM inference pass and, on some
samples, an additional LLM escalation pass. The observed latency advantage of the standalone
LLM therefore reflects deployment topology and orchestration effects rather than model
capacity alone.

The escalation behavior helps explain where the hybrid gains come from and where
it still leaves quality on the table. The routing run escalated 19 samples in
total: 17 because confidence fell below the threshold and 2 because the draft
answer could not be parsed. Among those escalations, 9 corrected an SLM error,
8 merely confirmed an answer that was already correct, 1 remained wrong, and 1
introduced a new error. At the same time, there were 5 non-escalated cases in
which the routing system retained the SLM answer even though the standalone
LLM answered correctly. In other words, the current threshold policy captures
several useful recoveries but also misses some salvageable cases.

EATS is informative in this comparison and behaves coherently across all three operating points.
The single-SLM baseline reaches the highest EATS at 0.488 because its strong
cost and energy savings slightly outweigh its accuracy loss under the current
accuracy-first formulation. The standalone LLM
baseline follows at 0.474, and the routing hybrid reaches 0.467 because its
two-stage control flow raises latency enough to offset some of its resource
savings. The scores are close, which is the right signal: none of the three
operating points dominates all dimensions. The more reliable interpretation
still comes from reading EATS together with accuracy, latency, cost, and
resource metrics.

\section{Currently Implemented Platform Capabilities}

The following pieces are already implemented and useful for development, smoke
testing, and early-stage experimentation:

\begin{itemize}
    \item the local control plane consisting of backend, frontend, MLflow integration, experiment runner, and result reporting;
    \item the canonical model alias system that maps architecture configurations to remote vLLM endpoints;
    \item baseline and hybrid architecture surfaces such as standalone inference, routing, multi-agent debate, and SLM voting ensembles;
    \item proxy-based and response-level metric propagation for latency, token counts, cost, energy, and emissions; and
    \item web surfaces for dashboarding, experiment launch, host visibility, result comparison, and playground-style prompt testing.
\end{itemize}

These capabilities are sufficient to support platform demonstrations,
architecture debugging, early pilot runs, and documentation of the end-to-end
workflow. They are not yet sufficient to support the final experimental claims
of the thesis.

\section{Components Requiring Further Validation}

Several core ideas of the thesis are still under construction or still require
methodological cleanup:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The coding benchmark is not yet finalized. The intended \texttt{custom\_stratified} benchmark should contain easy, medium, and hard coding tasks, but the current repository loader is still a legacy mixture and therefore cannot be treated as a valid coding benchmark.
    \item The project-specific \texttt{HumanEval} surface is conceptually defined as a human preference workflow, but the current repository file still contains a legacy prototype and is not the final benchmark implementation.
    \item The local end-to-end runner path still exhibits environment and benchmark-loader friction. In practice, this means that a repository-level ``run everything'' path is not yet stable enough to be cited as a finished validation pipeline.
    \item The project-proposed swarm family --- decentralized blackboard, entropy blackboard, and pure swarm --- is implemented as a promising architecture direction, but not yet supported by a finalized benchmark and valid comparative evidence.
\end{itemize}

For these reasons, the current experimental outputs are treated as exploratory
beyond the specific pilot comparisons reported above. No broader quantitative
claim is advanced in this report for benchmark configurations not yet finalized.

\section{Planned Coding Benchmark Protocol}

The main benchmark contribution planned for the thesis is a coding-focused
dataset organized by difficulty. The benchmark will be centered on the
\texttt{custom\_stratified} track and will compare architectures on practical
coding tasks rather than on a single public multiple-choice benchmark. The
target structure is shown in Table \ref{tab:coding-benchmark-plan}.

Each task record should contain at least an identifier, a difficulty label, a
prompt, optional starter code, public and hidden tests, a topic label, and
source metadata. The benchmark should support evaluation by exact output or
unit tests depending on the task. The main comparison axes will be:
architecture, SLM choice, LLM choice, escalation behavior, total cost, energy
consumption, carbon emissions, and EATS. This structure makes the benchmark
suitable for the thesis focus on coding skill rather than on general-purpose
language understanding alone.

\section{Thesis-Grade Evaluation Protocol}

Once the benchmark is finalized, the comparative protocol will proceed in three layers:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Standalone baseline:} a single stronger model answers every query and establishes the upper reference point for quality and the likely upper bound for cost.
    \item \textbf{Hybrid and ensemble comparison:} routing, multi-agent, and ensemble systems are compared against the standalone baseline under the same coding tasks and the same reporting stack.
    \item \textbf{Project-proposed architecture study:} the swarm family is evaluated after the benchmark and runner paths are stable enough to support valid comparison.
\end{itemize}

All comparisons will record accuracy or task success, latency, input/output tokens, total cost, API/infrastructure cost split, LLM-call ratio, energy, emissions, and EATS. Human preference evaluation will be added in a later phase through the UI-backed benchmark surface and should be treated as a complementary study rather than as a replacement for the coding benchmark.
