\section{Benchmark Suite and Evaluation Protocol}
\label{sec:bench-suite}

Five benchmarks are evaluated in this chapter. All runs use zero temperature. Accuracy is the proportion of samples for which the predicted answer matches the reference. EATS is computed from accuracy, normalized per-query cost, normalized average latency, and normalized per-query energy following the formula defined in Section~3 of this report; normalization is applied per benchmark against the mean of all standalone monolithic runs on that benchmark.

A methodological note applies throughout this chapter. Experiments recorded before per-step algorithmic latency instrumentation was active report wall-clock average latency, which includes HTTP round-trip and proxy overhead. Experiments recorded after that instrumentation became active report algorithmic latency, which is the sum of model-side inference durations only. The two sets of results are not directly comparable on the latency dimension; this distinction is flagged where it matters for interpretation. All other dimensions---accuracy, cost, energy, and CO$_2$---are unaffected.

\section{Architecture-Family Analysis}
\label{sec:arch-analysis}

\subsection{Standalone Baselines}

Standalone results confirm that model family, size, and quantization all contribute to benchmark performance in task-dependent ways. The MoE checkpoints \texttt{gemma4-26b-a4b} and \texttt{qwen3.5-35b-a3b} are particularly competitive: they match or exceed larger dense models on several benchmarks while operating at latencies of 2.8--7.4 seconds and costs of \$0.00185--\$0.00482 per query, yielding EATS of 0.519--0.738 across benchmarks. \texttt{llama3.3-70b} consistently achieves the lowest EATS among standalone models (\$0.01630--\$0.02034 per query) because its infrastructure cost dominates the EATS penalty term. \texttt{qwen3.5-27b} and \texttt{gemma4-31b} both achieve strong accuracy (88.6\% and 93.6\% on their respective peaks) but carry higher latency or cost penalties that limit their EATS to 0.317--0.529.

\texttt{gpt-oss-120b} and \texttt{gpt-oss-20b} perform well on MMLU (87.0\% and 86.6\%), ARC (95.4\% and 93.8\%), and TruthfulQA (82.4\% and 72.2\%) but diverge sharply on GSM8K, where accuracy falls to 67.8\% and 59.4\% respectively. This anomaly is discussed in Section~\ref{sec:exp-discussion}.

\subsection{Routing and Speculative Decoding}

Routing and speculative decoding using the \texttt{gemma4-4b}$\rightarrow$\texttt{gemma4-31b} pair produce the most prominent efficiency findings in this study. On the three benchmarks where algorithmic latency instrumentation was active during evaluation---ARC, HellaSwag, and TruthfulQA---routing achieves EATS of 0.884, 0.869, and 0.844 respectively. These values exceed the best standalone EATS on those benchmarks by 15--21 points. The improvement stems from combining near-monolithic accuracy with sub-2-second per-query latency and per-query costs below \$0.001. Speculative decoding achieves 1--5 percentage points higher accuracy than routing on these benchmarks at EATS values of 0.817--0.856, occupying the second-best EATS position in all three cases.

On MMLU, routing reports wall-clock average latency of 9.3 seconds (EATS 0.596) because the MMLU experiments predated per-step instrumentation. The wall-clock measure inflates the latency penalty relative to the newer runs. Speculative decoding on MMLU achieves 94.1\% accuracy---the highest of any configuration---but EATS is limited to 0.422 by the same wall-clock measurement condition.

On GSM8K, routing escalates only 1\% of queries to the LLM and achieves 95.0\% accuracy at EATS 0.560. This indicates that \texttt{gemma4-4b} is self-sufficient for nearly all GSM8K questions at the configured confidence threshold, reducing the LLM call overhead nearly entirely. The higher algorithmic latency (12.1 seconds) relative to ARC/HellaSwag/TruthfulQA reflects longer arithmetic reasoning output lengths rather than orchestration overhead.

\subsection{Active Oracle}

Active Oracle is evaluated on MMLU, ARC, and HellaSwag, achieving 74.0\%, 91.0\%, and 66.0\% accuracy with EATS of 0.304, 0.388, and 0.294. In all three cases it underperforms both routing and speculative decoding in accuracy and EATS, despite using the same SLM--LLM pair. The sub-query mechanism---which targets LLM consultation at specific knowledge gaps---incurs high wall-clock latency (21.4--30.6 seconds) and non-trivial sub-query costs that outweigh the benefit for multiple-choice benchmarks where targeted follow-up questions do not reliably correct the SLM's reasoning path.

\subsection{Ensemble}

The four-SLM ensemble (\texttt{gemma4-4b + llama3.2-3b + ministral3-3b + qwen3.5-4b}) is evaluated on all five benchmarks. Accuracy ranges from 64.0\% (TruthfulQA) to 96.0\% (GSM8K). The GSM8K result is the highest raw accuracy of any single configuration in the entire study, consistent with ensemble-diversity findings in the literature. However, EATS is limited to 0.279--0.387 across benchmarks because four parallel SLM calls generate latency of 15.6--21.6 seconds and combined energy that heavily penalizes the composite score. Single-SLM ensemble variants on MMLU (\texttt{ministral3-3b} at EATS 0.747, \texttt{qwen3.5-4b} at 0.438) show that a lean single-model ensemble can be highly efficient if the model has low latency and cost, though accuracy is limited.

\subsection{Pure Swarm}

Pure Swarm with \texttt{gemma4-4b} uses no heavy-model calls at inference time. It achieves 96.0\% on ARC, 95.0\% on GSM8K, 89.5\% on MMLU, 74.0\% on HellaSwag, and 72.0\% on TruthfulQA. The ARC result (96.0\%) is the highest accuracy of any configuration on that benchmark, exceeding every standalone model. The strong performance on ARC and GSM8K suggests that compact peer-agent consensus is effective for structured reasoning tasks with clear verifiable answers. EATS values of 0.245--0.410 are penalized by high wall-clock latency (21.4--27.5 seconds), which dominates the efficiency penalty term. Pure Swarm's lower scores on HellaSwag and TruthfulQA reflect that commonsense completion and factual calibration are harder for compact peer-agent coordination than science-knowledge or arithmetic tasks.

On the four benchmarks with algorithmic latency data, routing and speculative decoding occupy the top two EATS positions on three out of four (ARC, HellaSwag, TruthfulQA). On GSM8K, compact MoE standalone checkpoints take the top two positions because the routing LLM call ratio is 1\%---low enough that the SLM overhead from the initial draft pass adds latency without meaningful cost reduction relative to a lean MoE checkpoint. On MMLU, the EATS leader is the single-SLM \texttt{ministral3-3b} ensemble due to its exceptionally low cost per query, but its 57.0\% accuracy places it well outside the useful accuracy range for any deployment scenario.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:exp-discussion}

\paragraph{Hybrid architectures on the efficiency--quality frontier.}
The ARC, HellaSwag, and TruthfulQA results provide strong empirical support for the thesis hypothesis that routing and speculative decoding can achieve accuracy close to or matching the best standalone models while reducing per-query cost, latency, and CO$_2$ emission by factors of 2--15 relative to the same LLM running in standalone mode. This directly addresses RQ1 (can collaborative architectures match standalone LLM accuracy?) and RQ2 (what are the efficiency trade-offs?). EATS as a composite signal (RQ3) confirms the joint finding: hybrid architectures dominate the top two EATS positions on three of four benchmarks with consistent latency data, with values in the 0.817--0.884 range compared to 0.665--0.738 for the best standalone options.

\paragraph{GSM8K GPT-family anomaly.}
The low accuracy of \texttt{gpt-oss-120b} (67.8\%) and \texttt{gpt-oss-20b} (59.4\%) on GSM8K is inconsistent with their performance on all other benchmarks. The most likely explanation is answer-extraction sensitivity: GSM8K answers require identifying a numeric value from a free-form response, and GPT-family models may format their reasoning and final answers in ways that defeat the platform's numeric extraction heuristic. This is a measurement artifact rather than a true capability finding; dedicated output-parsing improvements and re-evaluation are planned before final thesis reporting.

\paragraph{Wall-clock versus algorithmic latency.}
MMLU routing experiments were recorded before per-step instrumentation was active and therefore carry wall-clock latency that inflates the EATS penalty relative to the newer algorithmic-latency runs. The full thesis evaluation will re-execute MMLU routing and speculative runs under the current instrumentation to obtain consistent latency data and valid EATS comparisons across all five benchmarks.

\paragraph{Pure Swarm capability ceiling.}
Pure Swarm demonstrates that compact SLM peer coordination can reach 96.0\% on ARC and 95.0\% on GSM8K with zero LLM calls, partially addressing RQ4 by matching multi-SLM collaboration findings from the reviewed literature. The design's wall-clock latency (21--27 seconds) currently prevents it from competing on EATS with well-instrumented hybrid architectures. Reducing swarm coordination latency is a natural optimization direction for future work.

\paragraph{Ensemble accuracy ceiling.}
The four-SLM ensemble achieves 96.0\% on GSM8K, the highest raw accuracy of any configuration, consistent with ensemble-diversity findings in the literature. EATS is limited to 0.387 because four parallel SLM calls carry a latency and energy penalty that the EATS formulation weights heavily. This result illustrates the fundamental trade-off the EATS metric is designed to quantify: accuracy gains from coordination are real but arrive at a cost that may not be acceptable in latency-sensitive deployments.

\section{Planned Extensions}
\label{sec:pending}

The current results cover five general-purpose language benchmarks. Two extensions are planned before the final thesis submission.

First, the primary thesis benchmark is a coding-focused dataset (\texttt{custom\_stratified}) organized by difficulty level (easy, medium, hard). Coding tasks provide verifiable correctness through automated unit-test evaluation and carry real deployment expectations around both answer quality and response latency, making them more directly relevant to the thesis research questions than multiple-choice benchmarks. Pilot coding runs have been completed, but the evaluation infrastructure for custom-task correctness scoring is still under development. The coding benchmark is targeted as the main comparison surface for the full thesis.

Second, three architecture families defined in the taxonomy---Debate (POA), Blackboard, and Entropy Blackboard---have not yet been benchmarked. These architectures are implemented in the repository but have not been included in the present evaluation. Incorporating them will complete the taxonomy comparison and provide empirical coverage for the full set of architecture families introduced in Chapter~3.
