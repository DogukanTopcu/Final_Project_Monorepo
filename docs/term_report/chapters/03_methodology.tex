\section{Research Design}
\label{sec:design}

\subsection{Research Questions}

This study investigates a systems-level research question that differs fundamentally from standard single-model benchmarking: \textit{which orchestration architecture achieves the best balance between answer quality and resource consumption under a controlled benchmark workload?} This central question is decomposed into the four empirical research questions stated in full in Section~\ref{sec:rqs} (RQ1--RQ4).

These questions require an architecture-comparative experimental design and mandate that accuracy and resource efficiency be treated as co-equal primary outcomes rather than relegating operational metrics to a secondary diagnostic role. RQ3 additionally motivates the introduction of EATS as a unified composite signal, and RQ4 ties the platform evaluation back to the findings of the companion systematic literature review.

\subsection{Architecture-as-Variable Experimental Design}

The primary independent variable in this study is the orchestration architecture, not the model. Standard NLP evaluation treats the model as the experimental unit and measures its accuracy on fixed benchmarks. The present study inverts that priority: the architecture is the treatment condition, and the models are components instantiated within each treatment. Two architectures may share the same underlying model checkpoints but differ substantially in how those models are composed, when each is invoked, and how their outputs are aggregated or verified.

This design choice has a direct methodological implication: for architecture comparisons to be valid, every architecture must receive the same input and return an output in the same format, so that differences in measured outcomes can be attributed to orchestration strategy rather than to differences in benchmark representation, prompt construction, or output parsing. The platform enforces this through a shared experimental contract described in Section~\ref{sec:contract}.

The \textbf{dependent variables} are task accuracy on the benchmark gold standard, average algorithmic latency per query (in seconds), total monetary cost per benchmark run (API plus infrastructure, in USD), total energy consumption (in kWh), estimated CO$_2$ emissions (in grams), and the Efficiency-Accuracy Trade-off Score (EATS) defined in Section~\ref{sec:eats}. The \textbf{controlled variables} are the benchmark dataset, the sample set (fixed by random seed), the prompt template, the answer parser, and the scoring policy. Temperature is held at 0.0 for all reported runs to eliminate output stochasticity as a source of measurement variance.

Four observations from the collaborative SLM--LLM literature directly shaped the platform design. First, recent systems treat \textbf{orchestration strategy}, not raw parameter count, as the principal lever for balancing capability and efficiency \cite{hao2024hybrid, shao2025dot, multiagent2025}. Second, a small set of \textbf{recurring collaboration patterns}---selective routing, verification, debate, and voting---accounts for the majority of designs, enabling a principled architecture taxonomy \cite{oh2025uncertainty, citer2025, debateagents2025, sentensemble2026}. Third, collaborative systems typically span \textbf{heterogeneous compute tiers} rather than a single uniform model server, so the evaluation infrastructure must reflect that topology to be ecologically valid \cite{hao2024hybrid, oh2025uncertainty, shao2025dot}. Fourth, an \textbf{evaluation gap} exists: cost and energy are reported far less frequently than accuracy \cite{slrmanuscript2026, energytoken2025}, which motivates a measurement-first design in which resource accounting is a primary outcome of every experiment. These four principles are operationalised through the architecture taxonomy (Section~\ref{sec:taxonomy}), the deployment topology (Section~\ref{sec:models}), and the evaluation methodology (Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}).

\section{System Architecture}
\label{sec:sysarch}

\subsection{Experimental Contract}
\label{sec:contract}

The foundation of the experimental design is a shared input--output contract enforced at the interface level. A benchmark loader normalises a raw dataset record into a \texttt{Query} object containing an identifier, question text, optional answer choices, the gold answer, and benchmark-specific metadata. An architecture consumes the \texttt{Query} using one or more model providers and returns a normalised \texttt{Response} object. The \texttt{Response} stores the predicted answer, a correctness flag, and a structured resource record containing latency, token counts (input and output, separated per inference step), total cost, energy in kWh, CO$_2$ emissions in grams, average GPU power, LLM-call count, and an ordered \texttt{inference\_steps} trace.

Because every architecture must conform to this contract, the experiment runner, scoring logic, energy annotation pipeline, and reporting layer are all architecture-agnostic. A new architecture is integrated by implementing the contract interface, not by modifying the evaluation infrastructure. This separation is essential for fair comparison: it prevents architecture-specific measurement artefacts from contaminating results.

\subsection{System Data Flow}

Figure~\ref{fig:dataflow} illustrates the end-to-end data flow for a single experiment run. A researcher submits a configuration specifying the target architecture, benchmark, sample count, random seed, confidence threshold (where applicable), and model aliases. The control plane validates model availability, resolves aliases to remote endpoints, and initiates the experiment runner. For each benchmark item, the runner: (1) normalises the dataset record into a \texttt{Query} and constructs the appropriate prompt; (2) passes the \texttt{Query} to the selected architecture, which issues one or more HTTP requests to remote inference endpoints according to its orchestration policy; (3) receives annotated responses from the metrics proxy, each carrying host-side latency, energy, and cost telemetry; (4) assembles the final \texttt{Response} from the orchestration outcome and step-level telemetry; and (5) evaluates correctness against the gold label and appends the result to the structured experiment log.

After all items are processed, the runner computes aggregate metrics---accuracy, mean algorithmic latency, total cost, total energy, total CO$_2$, and EATS---and writes a Markdown summary artefact. The MLflow tracking server stores per-item and aggregate metrics as structured experiment records for downstream cross-architecture comparison.

\subsection{Control Plane}

The control plane runs locally and comprises four components. (1) A \textbf{FastAPI backend} receives experiment configurations, validates model availability by querying remote health endpoints, launches runs, streams per-item progress, and persists results. (2) A \textbf{Next.js management interface} provides a consistent user interface for configuring, monitoring, and inspecting experiments; it is the operational interface of the experiment pipeline and not itself a methodological contribution. (3) The \textbf{experiment runner} is the core execution engine: it instantiates the selected architecture, drives the \texttt{Query}--\texttt{Response} cycle, evaluates correctness, and computes aggregate metrics. (4) An \textbf{MLflow tracking server} provides structured experiment logging; JSON and Markdown artefacts serve as the human-readable analysis layer.

\subsection{Inference Plane and Tool Selection Rationale}

The inference plane is remote and intentionally heterogeneous, mirroring the deployment reality observed in the reviewed literature: collaborative systems typically mix compact, always-available models with stronger, selectively invoked models hosted on more capable hardware \cite{hao2024hybrid, oh2025uncertainty, shao2025dot}. Three GPU tiers are deployed, each matched to the capability and availability requirements of its model class:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Google Cloud L4 instances (24 GB VRAM):} dedicated hosts for SLM serving. The L4's 24 GB VRAM is sufficient for 3B--5B parameter models, and its on-demand provisioning model provides predictable, low-latency availability suitable for high-frequency routing experiments. One L4 instance is assigned per SLM alias to eliminate queueing noise from shared host contention.
    \item \textbf{Google Cloud RTX PRO6000 instance (96 GB VRAM):} a shared host for mid-tier and MoE models in the 20B--40B range. These models are employed as mutually exclusive fallback or verification targets; no experiment requires two mid-tier models to run simultaneously. A host-lock mechanism in the control plane enforces this exclusivity to prevent cross-experiment latency and energy contamination.
    \item \textbf{Nebius H200 instance (141 GB VRAM):} a shared host for heavy-tier models (70B and 120B). The H200's high-bandwidth memory (HBM3e) is required to serve 70B+ parameter models at practical inference throughput; the same exclusivity constraint applies as on the RTX PRO6000 host.
\end{itemize}

All endpoints are served through vLLM \cite{kwon2023vllm}. vLLM was selected over alternatives such as TGI or Ollama because it provides: (a) memory-efficient KV-cache management through PagedAttention, which is essential for hosting models with substantially different parameter counts on the same physical host without per-request memory allocation overhead; (b) a uniform OpenAI-compatible \texttt{/v1/chat/completions} API across all supported model families, which ensures that the orchestration code remains provider-neutral and that architecture comparisons are not confounded by model-family-specific serving logic; and (c) support for diverse quantisation formats (FP16, FP4, NVFP4) needed to fit large models within the available VRAM.

A FastAPI-based metrics proxy is deployed on each host, interposed between the public endpoint and the vLLM process. The proxy intercepts each request, measures host-side inference duration, samples GPU power via NVIDIA NVML, estimates energy and CO$_2$ emissions, and appends a thesis-specific telemetry block to the OpenAI-compatible response before returning it to the control plane. This placement of the measurement boundary at the inference host is methodologically preferable to approximating energy from control-plane round-trip timing, because it captures the actual hardware power draw rather than an estimate that conflates network latency and orchestration overhead with inference cost \cite{energytoken2025}.

\section{Model Selection}
\label{sec:models}

\subsection{Tier-Based Stratification Rationale}

The model pool was assembled through a principled tier-based stratification strategy rather than through availability constraints alone. The literature review demonstrated that collaborative SLM--LLM systems derive their efficiency gains from the interaction between tiers; a meaningful architecture comparison therefore requires distinct tiers with clearly differentiated capability and cost profiles \cite{slrmanuscript2026, hao2024hybrid, oh2025uncertainty}.

Three additional criteria governed model selection within each tier. First, \textbf{model family diversity}: multiple families (Gemma, Qwen, LLaMA, Mistral, Phi) are represented so that architecture comparisons are not confounded by idiosyncrasies of a single model family's instruction-following or confidence calibration behaviour. Second, \textbf{conditional computation coverage}: two Mixture-of-Experts checkpoints (\texttt{gemma4-26b-a4b} and \texttt{qwen3.5-35b-a3b}) are included as standalone MoE baselines, enabling the present study to directly compare efficiency gains from internal expert sparsity with those arising from external orchestration---one of the principal conceptual boundaries identified in the literature review \cite{slrmanuscript2026}. Third, \textbf{reproducibility}: all selected models are open-weight checkpoints available through Hugging Face, ensuring that serving configurations are fully reproducible and independent of proprietary API agreements or access constraints.

\subsection{Deployment Topology Rationale}
\label{sec:topology}

The deployment topology follows the architectural principle observed in the reviewed literature: the low-cost tier should be stable and perpetually available, while the high-capability tier may be selectively and transiently invoked \cite{hao2024hybrid, oh2025uncertainty, shao2025dot}. Dedicated L4 hosts are assigned one-to-one with SLM aliases to eliminate queueing noise; this is particularly important for ensemble and swarm architectures that issue concurrent queries to multiple SLMs, where shared-host contention would introduce uncontrolled latency variance into the comparison.

The shared RTX PRO6000 and H200 hosts reflect the opposite design intent: mid-tier and heavy models are used as mutually exclusive fallback or verification targets, so collocating them on shared hosts is both economically efficient and methodologically coherent. The host-lock mechanism in the control plane enforces single-model exclusivity during active runs, preventing cross-experiment interference. The hourly infrastructure costs (\$0.75/h for L4, \$2.35/h for RTX PRO6000, \$6.50/h for H200) are direct inputs to the infrastructure cost component of EATS, ensuring that the efficiency metric reflects the actual economic cost structure of each architectural tier decision.

\section{Architecture Taxonomy}
\label{sec:taxonomy}

The architecture taxonomy was constructed by combining patterns extracted from the systematic literature review with novel architectural contributions introduced by the present project. The review demonstrated that most collaborative SLM--LLM systems can be grouped around a few recurring orchestration ideas: direct standalone baselines, selective hybrid collaboration, and multi-agent or multi-SLM coordination \cite{slrmanuscript2026, hao2024hybrid, shao2025dot, debateagents2025, sentensemble2026}. The architecture space is therefore organised into three categories: \textbf{Standalone}, \textbf{Hybrid}, and \textbf{Multi-SLM}. Within that taxonomy, some architectures were directly inspired by published patterns, while others represent novel architectural contributions introduced by the present study.

\subsection{Standalone Architectures}

\subsubsection{Standalone (Single-Model)}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{sources/standalone_simplified.png}
    \caption{Data flow and component interactions for the Standalone (Single-Model) architecture.}
    \label{fig:arch-standalone}
\end{figure}

The standalone single-model architecture is the simplest methodological baseline: the full query is submitted to a single model, which alone produces the final answer. No routing, verification, voting, or role decomposition occurs. In the present study, both heavy LLM-only runs and single-SLM runs are treated as standalone baselines because the decisive property is not model size but the absence of inter-model collaboration. This baseline is essential because the literature repeatedly compares hybrid and multi-agent systems against single-model references rather than against each other alone \cite{slrmanuscript2026, hao2024hybrid, multiagent2025}. Without a standalone baseline, it would be impossible to determine whether a collaborative design truly adds value or merely reproduces the behaviour of the stronger model with additional overhead.

\subsubsection{Standalone (MoE --- Mixture of Experts)}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{sources/standalone_moe_simplified.png}
    \caption{Data flow and component interactions for the Standalone (MoE) architecture.}
    \label{fig:arch-standalone-moe}
\end{figure}

MoE models are also treated as standalone baselines within the taxonomy of the present study, even though they implement conditional computation internally through sparse expert activation. The reason is methodological: from the experiment platform's perspective, an MoE checkpoint still receives one query and returns one response through a single model endpoint. Expert selection occurs inside the model weights rather than through an external orchestration policy. This makes the standalone MoE baseline an especially useful comparison surface. If an MoE model improves efficiency, that gain originates from internal sparse activation; if a collaborative architecture improves efficiency, the gain originates from system-level orchestration. By including checkpoints such as \texttt{gemma4-26b-a4b} and \texttt{qwen3.5-35b-a3b}, the present study can distinguish internal expert sparsity from external multi-model collaboration---one of the principal conceptual boundaries identified in the literature review \cite{slrmanuscript2026}.

\subsection{Hybrid Architectures}

\subsubsection{Routing}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{sources/routing_simplified.png}
    \caption{Data flow and component interactions for the Routing architecture.}
    \label{fig:arch-routing}
\end{figure}

Routing is the clearest query-level hybrid pattern in the present study. The compact model answers first; the system parses its answer and confidence; and the stronger model is invoked only if the compact model appears unreliable. This design was derived from the literature on uncertainty-aware hybrid inference and selective escalation, where the central question is whether a system can preserve most of the quality of a large model while paying for it only on difficult cases \cite{hao2024hybrid, oh2025uncertainty, citer2025, rethinkllm2025}. If the answer cannot be parsed, if the confidence falls below a threshold, or if other routing conditions are triggered, the full query is escalated to the LLM. This keeps the orchestration policy transparent and makes the LLM-call ratio directly interpretable. Methodologically, routing is the baseline hybrid architecture because it captures the simplest form of SLM--LLM collaboration.

\subsubsection{Speculative Decoding}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{sources/speculative_simplified.png}
    \caption{Data flow and component interactions for the Speculative Decoding architecture.}
    \label{fig:arch-speculative}
\end{figure}

Speculative Decoding was derived from token-level collaboration studies in which a smaller model proposes text and a stronger model selectively verifies or corrects it \cite{hao2024hybrid, oh2025uncertainty, citer2025}. In the platform, the architecture is implemented as a drafter--verifier system. The SLM first generates a compact draft; the verifier then checks the draft incrementally. If the verifier agrees with the current draft prefix, that prefix is accepted; on the first meaningful divergence, only the remaining suffix is rewritten by the stronger model. Because the present study uses heterogeneous open-weight models with different tokenizers, the implementation is a text-prefix approximation of speculative decoding rather than a tokenizer-coupled identical-token verifier. Even so, it preserves the core methodological idea from the literature: the large model should not regenerate the entire answer if the small model has already solved part of the response correctly.

\subsubsection{Debate (POA)}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{sources/multiagent_simplified.png}
    \caption{Data flow and component interactions for the Debate (POA) architecture.}
    \label{fig:arch-debate}
\end{figure}

Debate in the present study follows a Proponent--Opponent--Arbitrator (POA) structure. One agent proposes an answer, a second agent critiques that proposal, and an arbitrator produces the final decision. This pattern comes directly from the debate and collaborative-checking literature, which reports that role separation can improve reasoning discipline even when the participating models are individually weaker than a frontier baseline \cite{debateagents2025, resiliomate2025}. In the default configuration, the Proponent and Opponent are SLMs, while the Arbitrator can be either another SLM or a stronger LLM. The default configuration is classified as hybrid in the present study because the highest-quality setting typically uses an LLM arbitrator. Methodologically, POA matters because it tests whether accuracy gains can arise from adversarial internal critique rather than from direct fallback alone.

\subsubsection{Active Oracle}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{sources/active_oracle_simplified.png}
    \caption{Data flow and component interactions for the Active Oracle architecture.}
    \label{fig:arch-active-oracle}
\end{figure}

Active Oracle is a novel architectural contribution of the present project, motivated by the gap between simple routing and full debate. In many tasks, the SLM is not globally incapable; it is blocked only by one missing fact, one sub-calculation, or one uncertain rule. Routing the entire query to an LLM is then wasteful, while multi-round debate is often unnecessary. Active Oracle therefore keeps the SLM as the primary reasoner but allows it to emit an explicit \texttt{CALL\_ORACLE: <question>} request when it reaches an impasse. The LLM responds only to that narrow sub-question, and the SLM resumes reasoning from that point. Conceptually, this architecture draws on the broader literature on task decomposition, grounded support, and specialised helper roles \cite{shao2025dot, lee2024orchestrallm, alabbasi2024teleoracle}. Its core methodological purpose is to test whether sparse, targeted LLM interventions can close the quality gap more efficiently than full-query fallback.

\subsubsection{Blackboard}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{sources/blackboard_simplified.png}
    \caption{Data flow and component interactions for the Blackboard architecture.}
    \label{fig:arch-blackboard}
\end{figure}

Blackboard is a further project-developed architecture and represents a major structural departure from centralised routing. Instead of relying on a single router to assign queries, the system maintains a shared task board whose items cycle through states (\texttt{OPEN}, \texttt{IN\_PROGRESS}, \texttt{BLOCKED}, \texttt{RESOLVED}). Two SLM workers continuously inspect the board, compute bids based on predicted confidence and compute cost, and claim tasks whose expected utility exceeds a threshold. Task claiming supports two configurable policies: under \emph{first threshold}, the first worker whose bid clears the bid threshold claims the task immediately, while under \emph{highest bid}, the task is awarded by a deterministic auction to the top eligible bidder once all workers have posted their bids. Both policies are evaluated in Chapter~4. If no SLM claims a task before its time-to-live expires, a heavier LLM sweeper resolves it. Workers can also emit \texttt{SUB\_TASK} requests, causing the architecture to post a dependent subtask, block the parent, and resume it once the dependency is resolved. This design synthesises ideas from decomposition, asynchronous coordination, and resilient multi-agent execution found across the literature \cite{shao2025dot, multiagent2025, resiliomate2025, bioagents2025}. Methodologically, Blackboard tests whether decentralised coordination can replace a single global router while retaining a strong safety fallback.

\subsubsection{Entropy Blackboard}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{sources/blackboard_entropy_based_simplified.png}
    \caption{Data flow and component interactions for the Entropy Blackboard architecture.}
    \label{fig:arch-entropy-blackboard}
\end{figure}

Entropy Blackboard extends Blackboard by modifying only the bidding rule. In the standard Blackboard architecture, a worker's bid depends primarily on confidence minus a compute penalty. In Entropy Blackboard, the bid is additionally penalised by an entropy term derived from the worker's output token distribution, making the architecture less willing to let an apparently confident but internally uncertain SLM claim a difficult task. The design is directly motivated by uncertainty-aware hybrid inference studies that argue the real challenge is calibrating when a small model should abstain rather than merely detecting low confidence \cite{oh2025uncertainty}. Entropy Blackboard is therefore the uncertainty-sensitive decentralised architecture introduced by the present study: it preserves the same asynchronous task board as Blackboard but imposes a more conservative claim policy under distributional ambiguity.

\subsubsection{Ensemble}

\begin{figure}[htbp]

    \caption{Data flow and component interactions for the Ensemble architecture.}
    \label{fig:arch-ensemble}
\end{figure}

The Ensemble architecture was derived from the literature on voting, agreement, and zero-shot SLM collectives \cite{sentensemble2026, compos2024}. Multiple SLMs receive the same query in parallel, each produces one answer, and the system selects the final prediction through majority voting or confidence-weighted voting. An LLM tiebreaker is optionally available, but the core design intent is multi-SLM collaboration rather than hybrid escalation. Methodologically, Ensemble asks a direct question: if several compact models disagree, can their aggregate decision outperform any single compact model often enough to justify the additional inference cost?

\subsubsection{Pure Swarm}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{sources/blackboard_slms_simplified.png}
    \caption{Data flow and component interactions for the Pure Swarm architecture.}
    \label{fig:arch-pure-swarm}
\end{figure}

Pure Swarm is the strictest resource-constrained coordination design in the present study. It begins from the Blackboard idea but removes the heavy-model sweeper entirely. Two SLM peers bid on tasks and subtasks, and the system reduces the claim threshold after a task's time-to-live expires so that one peer eventually takes responsibility rather than deadlocking. This design deliberately pushes multi-SLM cooperation toward a more deployment-constrained regime, motivated by the broader multi-agent literature \cite{multiagent2025, resiliomate2025}. Methodologically, Pure Swarm provides the lower-bound counterpart to the hybrid family: rather than asking how selectively the system can invoke LLMs, it asks whether LLMs can be removed from the online path altogether without unacceptable quality loss.

\subsection{Methodological Implications of Chain of Thought (CoT) Reasoning}

A fundamental methodological assumption underlying these novel swarm and orchestration architectures is their inherent reliance on the Chain of Thought (CoT) reasoning capabilities of the underlying Small Language Models (SLMs). The integration of CoT serves not merely as an interpretability mechanism, but as an essential computational scratchpad that dictates the control flow of the multi-agent system \cite{multiagent2025, debateagents2025}.

\paragraph{CoT as a Control Mechanism in Swarm Architectures:} In the Blackboard, Entropy-Based Blackboard, and Pure Swarm frameworks, the step-by-step reasoning process is structurally bound to task decomposition. These architectures rely on the SLM's latent reasoning capacity to autonomously identify knowledge gaps and formulate explicit dependency requests (e.g., via a \texttt{SUB\_TASK:} directive) \cite{lee2024orchestrallm, chen2025collabmech}. Consequently, without the intermediate token generation afforded by CoT, the models would lack the necessary computational space to trigger multi-agent collaboration, rendering the decentralized problem-solving paradigm inoperable.

\paragraph{Active Oracle Optimization and CoT Minimization:} While CoT enables complex decomposition, it introduces significant inference overhead, directly degrading latency and increasing token generation costs. To optimize the Efficiency-Accuracy Trade-off Score (EATS), a recent methodological refinement was introduced to the Active Oracle architecture \cite{energytoken2025}. Initially dependent on exhaustive CoT before emitting a \texttt{CALL\_ORACLE:} request, the prompt design was constrained to mandate only brief reasoning (e.g., limiting the model's internal reflection to a single sentence) \cite{shao2025dot}.

\paragraph{Empirical Impact:} By curtailing the reliance on protracted CoT, the Active Oracle minimizes token bloat while preserving the architectural capability to halt execution and consult the Heavy LLM for factual precision. Preliminary empirical results demonstrate a substantial improvement in the EATS metric \cite{slrmanuscript2026}. This optimization sustains the high accuracy bounds provided by the Oracle while drastically mitigating the algorithmic latency, time-to-first-token, and economic cost previously amortized over verbose SLM reasoning steps \cite{alabbasi2024teleoracle}.

\section{Evaluation Methodology}
\label{sec:evaluation}

\subsection{Metric Selection Rationale}

The evaluation methodology treats resource efficiency as a co-equal primary outcome alongside accuracy. This choice is directly motivated by the evaluation gap identified in the systematic literature review: the majority of studies on collaborative SLM--LLM systems report accuracy as the headline metric while omitting or underreporting latency, cost, and energy \cite{slrmanuscript2026, energytoken2025}. Restricting evaluation to accuracy would make it impossible to answer the central research questions of this study, since RQ1--RQ3 all concern the quality--efficiency trade-off rather than accuracy alone.

Accordingly, every \texttt{Response} object records five primary outcome dimensions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Accuracy}: the fraction of benchmark items for which the architecture produces a correct answer, as determined by the benchmark-specific gold label and answer parser.
    \item \textbf{Algorithmic latency}: the sum of model-side inference latencies across all steps of a single query, in seconds (Section~\ref{sec:latency}).
    \item \textbf{Total monetary cost}: the sum of direct API cost and infrastructure cost, in USD (Section~\ref{sec:energy}).
    \item \textbf{Energy consumption}: total electrical energy drawn by the GPU(s) during inference, in kWh (Section~\ref{sec:energy}).
    \item \textbf{Carbon emissions}: estimated CO$_2$-equivalent emissions in grams, derived from energy consumption and a grid carbon intensity factor (Section~\ref{sec:energy}).
\end{itemize}

In addition to these five stored dimensions, result tables in Chapter~4 report \textbf{LLM\,\%}: the fraction of queries in a run that invoked the heavy language model at least once, derived from the per-query LLM-call count stored in each \texttt{Response}. For standalone architectures this value is always 100\%; for Multi-SLM architectures it is always 0\%.

These five dimensions are synthesised into the composite Efficiency-Accuracy Trade-off Score (EATS), described in Section~\ref{sec:eats}.

\subsection{Latency Semantics}
\label{sec:latency}

The project distinguishes between \textbf{wall-clock latency} and \textbf{algorithmic latency}. Wall-clock latency is the full observed duration from the caller's perspective, including HTTP round trips, proxy work, and local orchestration overhead. Algorithmic latency---the primary latency used in aggregate metrics---is the sum of the actual model-side inference latencies reported by the provider or by step-level instrumentation. If a model endpoint exposes a server-side latency value, that value is preferred. If not, the platform sums the latencies of the recorded inference steps. Only when neither source is available does the system fall back to total wall-clock duration. This distinction is methodologically important because the present study compares architectures, not incidental network noise. A routing architecture should not appear artificially worse than a standalone baseline merely because its measurement path includes extra proxy bookkeeping or orchestration overhead.

\subsection{EATS: Efficiency-Accuracy Trade-off Score}
\label{sec:eats}

To jointly compare answer quality and resource consumption, the present study introduces the Efficiency-Accuracy Trade-off Score (EATS). Let $\alpha \in [0,1]$ denote the architecture's task accuracy, $C \geq 0$ its total monetary cost per run (USD), $L \geq 0$ its mean algorithmic latency per query (s), and $E \geq 0$ its total energy consumption (kWh). The corresponding benchmark-specific standalone baseline values are $C_0$, $L_0$, and $E_0$, all strictly positive by construction. Three dimensionless normalisation ratios are defined as:

\begin{align}
    \tilde{C} = \frac{C}{C_0}, \qquad
    \tilde{L} = \frac{L}{L_0}, \qquad
    \tilde{E} = \frac{E}{E_0}.
    \label{eq:norm-components}
\end{align}

The tilde notation denotes a dimensionless ratio, following the convention used in ML efficiency literature for normalised quantities; a circumflex ($\hat{\cdot}$) is reserved for estimated quantities in the statistical sense and is not used here. EATS combines a weighted efficiency penalty $P_{\text{eff}}$ with an explicit accuracy-deficit penalty $P_{\text{acc}}$:

\begin{align}
    P_{\text{eff}} &= 0.65\,\tilde{C} \;+\; 0.20\,\tilde{L} \;+\; 0.15\,\tilde{E}, \label{eq:penalty} \\[4pt]
    P_{\text{acc}} &= 1 - \alpha, \label{eq:accdeficit} \\[4pt]
    \text{EATS} &= \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \lambda\,P_{\text{eff}} + \beta\,P_{\text{acc}}}, \label{eq:eats}
\end{align}

where the efficiency scale $\lambda = 0.40$ and the accuracy-deficit scale $\beta = 0.60$ satisfy $\lambda + \beta = 1$, and the efficiency weights $(0.65, 0.20, 0.15)$ also sum to one so that $P_{\text{eff}} = 1$ at the baseline operating point.

Three structural properties follow directly from these definitions and should be noted explicitly.

First, \textbf{baseline identity}: when Eq.~\eqref{eq:eats} is applied to the standalone baseline itself, $\tilde{C} = \tilde{L} = \tilde{E} = 1$ by construction, so $P_{\text{eff}} = 1$ identically, and therefore:
\[
  \text{EATS}_{\text{baseline}} = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \lambda + \beta(1-\alpha)} = \frac{\alpha}{1 + \lambda\alpha} \quad \text{(using } \lambda + \beta = 1\text{)}.
\]
The baseline EATS is thus a function of accuracy alone, providing a clean reference curve against which every collaborative architecture can be placed.

Second, \textbf{monotonicity}: writing the denominator as $D = (1-\beta)\alpha + \lambda P_{\text{eff}} + \beta$, we have $\partial\,\text{EATS}/\partial\alpha = (\lambda P_{\text{eff}} + \beta)/D^2 > 0$ and $\partial\,\text{EATS}/\partial P_{\text{eff}} = -\lambda\alpha/D^2 < 0$, confirming that EATS is strictly increasing in accuracy and strictly decreasing in the efficiency penalty.

Third, \textbf{domain}: because $\beta > 0$, the denominator satisfies $D \geq \beta > 0$ for all $\alpha \in [0,1]$ even when accuracy and all efficiency penalties vanish, so EATS is well-defined on the entire domain. This is a deliberate improvement over a bare $\alpha/(\alpha+P_{\text{eff}})$ form, which is undefined at $\alpha = P_{\text{eff}} = 0$; the accuracy-deficit term removes that singularity.

The structure $\alpha/(\alpha + \lambda P_{\text{eff}} + \beta P_{\text{acc}})$ is reminiscent of the F-score formulation, where the penalty terms act as resource-consumption and residual-error counterparts to accuracy; the analogy is motivational rather than exact, since $P_{\text{eff}}$ is a weighted sum of normalised ratios that need not lie in $[0,1]$. Unlike a multiplicative penalty, the additive form degrades gracefully when one efficiency dimension is only approximately measured---a multiplicative structure would collapse EATS toward zero if any single component were unmeasured or near zero.

The efficiency weights (0.65 for cost, 0.20 for latency, 0.15 for energy) are an explicit design choice reflecting a cost-primary prioritisation: monetary cost is the most immediately actionable efficiency constraint for practitioners; latency ranks second because it directly governs interactive responsiveness; energy is weighted lowest because it is currently the dimension most subject to measurement approximation in the present platform. The outer scales $\lambda = 0.40$ and $\beta = 0.60$ govern how strongly resource cost and residual inaccuracy are penalised relative to delivered accuracy; the accuracy-deficit term $\beta(1-\alpha)$ prevents a cheap but low-accuracy architecture from scoring near 1.0 on resource efficiency alone. These values are not empirically derived from the literature; they encode a specific deployment-context judgment.

To verify that the architecture comparison does not hinge on the precise weights, a sensitivity analysis was run over a grid of efficiency weights within $\pm 0.1$ of the default (step 0.05, subject to $w_C + w_L + w_E = 1$) crossed with $\lambda \in [0.30, 0.50]$---95 weight settings per benchmark. Across all settings and all five benchmarks the metric never reversed a Pareto-dominating pair (270 of 270 dominance pairs respected): whenever one configuration is at least as accurate as another while costing no more in money, latency, and energy, EATS ranks it higher. The top-ranked configuration was unchanged in 94\% of the weight settings and the top-three set in 88\%, with a mean Kendall $\tau_b$ of 0.94 between each perturbed ranking and the default ranking. The only benchmark whose top-three set is sensitive is GSM8K, where the leading configurations lie within 0.03 EATS of one another, so perturbations reshuffle near-tied positions without displacing the top-ranked configuration. The default weights therefore lie in a stable interior of the weight space, and the architecture-level conclusions in Chapter~4 are robust to the weight choice.

EATS does not replace raw accuracy; it provides a single composite ranking signal for the quality--efficiency trade-off identified as systematically underreported in the literature \cite{slrmanuscript2026, energytoken2025}.

\subsection{Energy, Carbon, and Cost Measurement}
\label{sec:energy}

The VM-side metrics proxy is the primary measurement instrument. When a request arrives at the proxy endpoint, the proxy records a high-resolution start time, forwards the request to the local vLLM process, and upon receiving the response computes an experiment-specific telemetry block. Energy is estimated using two mechanisms in priority order: (1) \textbf{CodeCarbon}, the preferred software-level estimator; (2) \textbf{NVIDIA NVML}, accessed through \texttt{pynvml} to sample the instantaneous GPU power draw at the host level, used as a fallback by integrating mean power over the measured inference duration. The following notation is used throughout: $P_{\text{GPU}} \geq 0$ denotes the mean GPU power in watts reported by NVML during a single inference step; $\Delta s \geq 0$ denotes the NVML-measured inference duration in seconds, converted to hours as $t = \Delta s\,/\,3600$; $r \geq 0$ denotes the hourly cloud-instance rate in USD/h; $C_{\text{API}} \geq 0$ denotes the direct token-level cost charged by the model provider in USD (computed from the per-token price and the input/output token counts returned by the endpoint); and $k$ denotes the grid carbon intensity factor in gCO$_2$/kWh. The per-step resource estimates are:

\begin{align}
    E \;[\text{kWh}]            &= \frac{P_{\text{GPU}}}{1\,000} \times t
                                  \;=\; \frac{P_{\text{GPU}} \cdot \Delta s}{3\,600\,000},     \label{eq:energy} \\[4pt]
    \text{CO}_2 \;[\text{g}]    &= k \times E,                                                \label{eq:co2}    \\[4pt]
    C_{\text{infra}} \;[\$]     &= r \times t,                                                \label{eq:infra}  \\[4pt]
    C \;[\$]                    &= C_{\text{API}} + C_{\text{infra}}.                         \label{eq:cost}
\end{align}

The dimensional consistency of Eq.~\eqref{eq:energy} follows from $(\text{W}/1000) \times (\text{s}/3600) = \text{kW}\cdot\text{h} = \text{kWh}$. The current default value is $k = 380\;\text{gCO}_2/\text{kWh}$, which is the approximate European average grid carbon intensity \cite{energytoken2025}. Since the GCP and Nebius data-centre regions used in this study are not exclusively European, this factor is applied as an indicative estimate; actual per-kWh emissions depend on regional grid composition, which varies across cloud provider locations. Additionally, cloud providers frequently procure renewable energy certificates that substantially reduce the effective emission factor. The thesis therefore treats CO$_2$ estimates as indicative rather than precise values, as further discussed in Section~\ref{sec:threats}.

A resource-profile fallback layer handles multi-step architectures and endpoints that expose only partial proxy telemetry. Each model alias is mapped to a host profile containing an hourly infrastructure price and an approximate GPU power value. Current profile values are: L4 at \$0.75/h and 72~W, RTX PRO6000 at \$2.35/h and 320~W, and H200 at \$6.50/h and 600~W. This fallback mechanism does not claim measurement equivalence with direct proxy readings; it provides a consistent experiment-wide accounting policy so that multi-step architectures can still be compared fairly at the aggregate level.

\subsection{Baseline Normalisation}
\label{sec:normalisation}

EATS normalisation is tied to benchmark-specific standalone baselines. The platform maintains a registry of baseline runs that record full-LLM cost, model-side latency, and total energy for a given benchmark, sample size, seed, and model alias. Architecture-level runs are normalised against these values rather than against handwritten constants. This is methodologically important because a benchmark such as GSM8K has a very different latency and token profile from HellaSwag or ARC-Challenge. Using benchmark-local standalone references ensures that the efficiency penalty component of EATS reflects the actual difficulty and output characteristics of the workload under evaluation.

Two normalisation modes are formally defined. The \emph{per-experiment mode} normalises each architecture run against the registered full-LLM baseline for the exact same benchmark, sample count, and seed; this is the default mode used by the experiment runner and the MLflow tracking layer for live experiment comparison. The \emph{aggregate mode}, used for the cross-architecture result tables in Chapter~4, re-normalises using the mean of all standalone monolithic runs on a given benchmark as the common reference point; this ensures that heterogeneous standalone, hybrid, ensemble, and swarm configurations can be placed on the same per-benchmark comparison scale when multiple standalone configurations are available. Both modes use the same EATS formula (Eq.~\eqref{eq:eats}); they differ only in which standalone reference values are substituted for $C_0$, $L_0$, and $E_0$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:norm-components}.

\section{Benchmark Selection}
\label{sec:benchmarks}

The benchmark suite was selected to cover the capability dimensions emphasised by the systematic literature review: broad knowledge, structured reasoning, commonsense continuation, arithmetic reasoning, and truthfulness \cite{slrmanuscript2026}. The final benchmark set consists of \texttt{mmlu}, \texttt{arc\_challenge}, \texttt{hellaswag}, \texttt{gsm8k}, and \texttt{truthfulqa}. Together they constitute a multi-dimensional evaluation suite spanning knowledge breadth, structured reasoning, commonsense inference, arithmetic reasoning, and adversarial truthfulness, on which routing, verification, debate, and multi-SLM coordination can be tested under one shared measurement policy.

\textbf{MMLU} \cite{hendrycks2021mmlu} serves as the main general-reasoning benchmark, covering 57 academic and professional subject areas in zero-shot multiple-choice form. Its \texttt{subject} field additionally enables domain-level disaggregation of accuracy, making it possible to ask whether a hybrid architecture fails systematically in specific disciplines. \textbf{ARC-Challenge} \cite{clark2018arc} (referred to as ARC in tables) contributes a harder science-reasoning surface and is particularly useful for architectures such as routing and debate because it pressures the system to distinguish between cases that are resolvable by an SLM and those that require stronger reasoning support. \textbf{HellaSwag} \cite{zellers2019hellaswag} evaluates commonsense continuation and tests whether collaboration mechanisms genuinely improve reasoning or merely amplify stylistic fluency, since all candidate completions remain superficially plausible. \textbf{GSM8K} \cite{cobbe2021gsm8k} provides open-ended arithmetic reasoning problems, which are central to the present study's energy measurement interest: smaller models can become energy-inefficient when they compensate for limited capability by generating lengthy chain-of-thought traces \cite{energytoken2025}. \textbf{TruthfulQA} \cite{lin2022truthfulqa} extends the evaluation surface toward adversarial self-correction, asking whether architectures such as debate and oracle consultation help the system avoid persuasive falsehoods rather than merely improving answer matching.

Methodologically, the suite supports two kinds of comparison: within-benchmark architecture comparison, where the same workload is used to compare standalone, hybrid, and multi-SLM architectures under a fixed prompt and scoring policy; and cross-benchmark behaviour analysis, where the present study asks whether an architecture's efficiency advantage on one workload persists when the task shifts from broad knowledge to arithmetic or truthfulness. The literature consistently shows that collaborative gains are task-sensitive rather than universal \cite{slrmanuscript2026, debateagents2025, sentensemble2026}, which is why the present study does not treat any single benchmark as sufficient evidence for an architecture claim. Full details of the benchmark standardisation procedure---prompt templates, answer parsers, and sampling protocol---are provided in Chapter~4.

\section{Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:threats}

\subsection{Internal Validity}

Four threats to internal validity are acknowledged. First, \textbf{shared-host measurement contamination}: on shared GPU hosts, operating-system-level background processes can affect GPU power readings and inference latency even when the host-lock mechanism prevents concurrent model switches. This threat is mitigated but not eliminated by the exclusivity enforcement in the control plane. Second, \textbf{routing threshold sensitivity}: the confidence threshold used in routing architectures (0.8 in reported runs) was set heuristically and has not yet been subjected to a systematic sensitivity analysis. Different thresholds produce different LLM-call ratios and therefore different cost and latency outcomes; the reported results represent one operating point on a continuous trade-off curve rather than an optimised configuration. Third, \textbf{energy measurement uncertainty}: the energy estimator combines CodeCarbon software estimation and NVML GPU power sampling, both of which introduce uncertainty. CodeCarbon may under- or over-estimate depending on process isolation level; NVML sampling at discrete intervals can miss short-duration power spikes. These are inherent limitations of software-based energy measurement in multi-process server environments \cite{energytoken2025}. Fourth, \textbf{sample size}: configurations evaluated with 100 samples per benchmark provide sufficient directional insights but insufficient data for statistically rigorous comparisons; standalone reference runs use larger samples (500), but per-configuration hybrid and multi-SLM results are based on 100-sample batches. No confidence intervals or significance tests are reported; all results in this report are treated as exploratory evidence rather than definitive claims.

\subsection{External Validity}

Two threats to external validity are acknowledged. First, \textbf{model family generalisability}: the reported routing and speculative decoding experiments use models exclusively from the Gemma family (\texttt{gemma4-4b} and \texttt{gemma4-31b}). Routing effectiveness, escalation rates, and the energy efficiency of different architectures may vary substantially across model families with different instruction-following calibration or confidence expression behaviour. Extending the evaluation to multiple model families across all tiers is identified as future work. Second, \textbf{benchmark generalisability}: the five benchmarks all use multiple-choice or fixed numeric-answer formats. Efficiency and quality rankings observed on broad-knowledge, commonsense, arithmetic, and truthfulness tasks may not transfer directly to open-ended generation or domain-specific instruction-following scenarios.

\subsection{Construct Validity}

Two threats to construct validity are acknowledged. First, \textbf{EATS weight sensitivity}: the efficiency penalty weights (0.65 for cost, 0.20 for latency, 0.15 for energy), together with the outer efficiency and accuracy-deficit scales ($\lambda = 0.40$, $\beta = 0.60$), reflect one specific prioritisation of deployment concerns. Alternative weight configurations---for instance, prioritising latency over cost in latency-critical applications---shift the EATS values, but the sensitivity analysis reported in Section~\ref{sec:eats} shows the architecture rankings are largely robust to $\pm 0.1$ perturbations of these weights: the top-ranked configuration is unchanged in 94\% of the grid and no Pareto-dominating pair is ever reversed. Second, \textbf{carbon intensity assumption}: the fixed grid factor of 380~gCO$_2$/kWh is a European average and may not accurately reflect the actual carbon intensity of the specific cloud data-centre regions used. Cloud providers frequently procure renewable energy certificates, which could substantially lower the effective emission factor. The present study therefore treats CO$_2$ estimates as indicative rather than precise values, and the absolute emissions figures should not be interpreted as certified environmental measurements.
