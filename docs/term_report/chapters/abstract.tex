\chapter*{Abstract}

This term report presents a thesis experiment platform for comparing small language model (SLM) and large language model (LLM) orchestration architectures under a shared evaluation contract. Instead of evaluating isolated models, the platform compares how different architectures that receive the same \texttt{Query} input and return the same \texttt{Response} output trade off answer quality against latency, token usage, cost, energy consumption, carbon emissions, and the Efficiency-Accuracy Trade-off Score (EATS). The system combines a local control plane, remote OpenAI-compatible vLLM endpoints across GPU tiers, MLflow tracking, JSON/Markdown reporting, and a measurement-first layer that propagates latency, cost, \texttt{energy\_kwh}, and \texttt{co2\_g} through the experiment pipeline.

The report presents two pilot comparisons rather than a final thesis result set. On MMLU, a standalone \texttt{gemma4-31b} run achieved 85\% accuracy, while a routing configuration with \texttt{gemma4-4b} as primary responder and selective escalation to \texttt{gemma4-31b} reached 76\%; the routing design reduced heavy-model invocations to 34\% of queries and lowered energy and CO$_2$, but incurred higher latency. On GSM8K, a same-family three-way comparison placed routing at 91\% accuracy between the single-SLM baseline at 83\% and the standalone LLM baseline at 95\%, while reducing cost by approximately 25\% and energy by approximately 42\% relative to the standalone LLM baseline. These results are presented as pilot evidence of measurable quality-efficiency trade-offs rather than as final thesis claims.
