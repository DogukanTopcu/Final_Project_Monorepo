\chapter*{Abstract}

Large language models (LLMs) achieve state-of-the-art performance on reasoning and language understanding tasks but impose substantial inference costs in latency, energy, and monetary expenditure. Recent research explores collaborative architectures that combine small language models (SLMs) with LLMs through routing, speculative decoding, ensemble coordination, and decentralized swarm strategies to reduce these costs. However, the field still lacks a systematic evaluation framework that treats cost and energy as first-class outcomes alongside accuracy. This gap was identified through a companion systematic literature review of 54 collaborative SLM--LLM studies conducted under a PRISMA 2020-guided protocol, and it directly motivated the measurement-first design of the platform presented in this report.

The platform executes every architecture under a shared contract that records accuracy, latency, cost, energy, and carbon for every query. A composite metric, EATS (Efficiency-Adjusted Task Score), is introduced to rank architectures jointly on quality and efficiency. The experimental study evaluates six architecture families---standalone, routing, speculative decoding, active oracle, ensemble, and pure swarm---across five public benchmarks. On MMLU, routing reaches 76.7\% accuracy while limiting heavy-model invocations to 48.7\% of queries, and speculative decoding reaches 86.0\%. On ARC, HellaSwag, and TruthfulQA, routing and speculative decoding remain near the top of the efficiency--quality frontier, achieving accuracy within a few percentage points of the best standalone models while reducing per-query cost and energy by substantial margins. Pure Swarm reaches the highest accuracy on one benchmark using no heavy-model calls, while multi-SLM ensembles achieve the highest raw accuracy on another, at the cost of elevated latency. EATS provides a stable ranking signal that captures these trade-offs in a single score.

The current evaluation covers general-purpose language benchmarks. Planned extensions include a coding-focused benchmark organized by difficulty and the remaining architecture families defined in the taxonomy.
