\chapter*{Abstract}

Large language models (LLMs) deliver strong capabilities on reasoning and coding tasks but impose substantial inference costs in latency, energy, and monetary expenditure. While recent work explores collaborative architectures that combine small language models (SLMs) with LLMs through routing, verification, debate, and decentralized coordination, the research community still lacks a systematic evaluation framework that treats cost and energy as first-class outcomes alongside accuracy. This gap was identified through a companion systematic literature review of 54 collaborative SLM--LLM studies, which directly motivated the measurement-first design of the present platform.

The report presents two pilot comparisons rather than a final thesis result set. On MMLU, a standalone \texttt{gemma4-31b} run achieved 85\% accuracy, while a routing configuration with \texttt{gemma4-4b} as primary responder and selective escalation to \texttt{gemma4-31b} reached 76\%; the routing design reduced heavy-model invocations to 34\% of queries and lowered energy and CO$_2$, but incurred higher latency. On GSM8K, a same-family three-way comparison placed routing at 91\% accuracy between the single-SLM baseline at 83\% and the standalone LLM baseline at 95\%, while reducing cost by approximately 25\% and energy by approximately 42\% relative to the standalone LLM baseline. These results are presented as pilot evidence of measurable quality-efficiency trade-offs rather than as final thesis claims.

\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} small language models, large language models, orchestration architectures, energy-aware evaluation, efficiency-accuracy trade-off
