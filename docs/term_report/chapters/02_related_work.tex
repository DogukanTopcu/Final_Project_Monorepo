\section{The Capability-Efficiency Trade-off in Language Models}
Large language models (LLMs) deliver strong reasoning, coding, and generation performance, but their hardware and deployment requirements often confine them to cloud infrastructure \cite{egi2025, shao2025dot}. This creates a bottleneck in real-world deployments, raising significant concerns regarding inference latency, API cost, and energy demand \cite{energytoken2025, hao2024hybrid}. Furthermore, the scale of this energy demand translates to a substantial carbon footprint, reinforcing the need for environmentally sustainable AI practices \cite{energytoken2025, arasum2025}. Small language models (SLMs) have emerged as an attractive alternative for local deployment, offering lower-cost, data-sovereign, and ecologically efficient operation \cite{slmreview2025prisma, wang2024slmsurvey}.

However, standalone SLMs consistently trail frontier LLMs on broad reasoning tasks and scenarios requiring extensive contextual knowledge \cite{wang2025collabperf}. The literature indicates that simply forcing a smaller model to execute complex reasoning workflows does not inherently solve the efficiency problem. For example, using Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting on an SLM can substantially increase its per-query energy use, sometimes rendering a directly prompted LLM more energy-efficient than an SLM generating a long reasoning trace \cite{energytoken2025}. This task-dependent relationship between model size and energy consumption informs orchestration strategies that balance computational overhead with reasoning accuracy.

While standalone architectures traditionally process queries through dense layers, conditional computation via Mixture of Experts (MoE) provides a distinct method for optimizing token-level efficiency \cite{wang2025collabperf}. MoE frameworks employ sparse gating mechanisms to activate a restricted subset of expert networks per token, minimising the computational footprint of high-parameter models during inference \cite{wang2025collabperf}. In contemporary literature, these internal gating mechanics are typically examined within hybrid edge-cloud load balancing systems or localized submodel tuning pipelines \cite{wang2025collabperf}. However, because internal weight-level expert selection operates independently of external orchestration policies, a gap remains in directly comparing these sparse standalone baselines against multi-model collaborative architectures. This supports the inclusion of MoE checkpoints as standalone single-model controls to explicitly decouple internal algorithmic sparsity from system-level orchestration.

\section{Orchestration Strategies for Collaborative Systems}
To address the limitations of standalone single-model deployments, recent literature demonstrates a clear shift toward collaborative systems \cite{chen2025collabmech, wang2025collabperf}. These systems increasingly treat the large model as a selectively invoked capability rather than the default engine for every query \cite{chen2025collabmech, wang2025collabperf}.

\subsection{Hybrid Escalation and Routing Mechanisms}
Hybrid architectures optimize the trade-off between capability and efficiency by routing only difficult or uncertain cases to larger models \cite{hao2024hybrid, qwqrouting2025}. This selective-routing principle instantiates difficulty-aware or quality-latency-aware query routing, in which an SLM serves as the primary first-pass evaluator.

At the token level, collaboration frequently employs speculative decoding, where an SLM drafts candidate tokens that a stronger remote verifier approves in bulk \cite{hao2024hybrid, oh2025uncertainty}. If the draft is rejected, the system performs a partial rollback, instructing the LLM to rewrite the sequence from the point of failure. This draft-verification process substantially reduces average latency compared to full LLM inference while maintaining high accuracy on confident queries. Furthermore, frameworks employing uncertainty-aware skipping allow confident SLM tokens to bypass the remote LLM entirely, resulting in significantly faster token throughput \cite{oh2025uncertainty, fedhlm2025}.

At the query level, instance-level model selection delegates simpler, structured tasks to the SLM and reserves the LLM for complex escalation \cite{rethinkllm2025}. This concept inspires active oracle frameworks, where the SLM operates as the primary step-by-step reasoner but leverages semantic routing to trigger synchronous fallbacks to a heavy LLM strictly for factual lookups or complex sub-calculations. By reserving the LLM for targeted escalation rather than using it as the default reasoner, these routing architectures can reduce total token consumption and operational costs while preserving strong task performance \cite{qwqrouting2025, alabbasi2024teleoracle}.

\subsection{Multi-Agent and Role-Specialized Frameworks}
Multi-agent architectures decompose complex tasks into sub-tasks managed by specialized language agents, reducing the burden placed on a single model's zero-shot capabilities \cite{multiagent2025, shao2025dot, pacheco2025flight}. Frameworks employing specific agent roles, such as the Assistant--Checker dynamic, have been shown to reduce computational redundancy and improve task-level verification through structured multi-agent feedback loops \cite{resiliomate2025}. Furthermore, architectures employing adversarial debate---structuring interactions via Proponent, Opponent, and Arbitrator roles---have demonstrated accuracy gains in mathematical reasoning while maintaining bounded token budgets \cite{debateagents2025}. These role-specialized interactions show that structured collaboration can compensate, at least partially, for the limited parameter counts of individual small models.

\subsection{Ensembles and Aggregation}
Ensemble architectures can mitigate the error rates of individual language models by aggregating multiple zero-shot or fine-tuned inferences \cite{sentensemble2026, compos2024}. Techniques such as majority voting across disparate SLM backbones have been shown to rival the performance of proprietary LLMs in tasks such as sentiment analysis \cite{sentensemble2026}, while role- and prompt-diverse ensemble pipelines have also improved hallucination detection through multi-model aggregation and majority voting \cite{compos2024}. Advanced ensemble mechanisms further optimize this process by using performance-weighted voting or expert-selected networks of experts, improving robustness in specialized classification settings while preserving computational efficiency \cite{tcm2025, textnex2025}.

\section{Decentralization and Swarm Orchestration}
While hierarchical multi-agent systems demonstrate strong performance, they frequently rely on static topologies or centralized controllers. These centralized architectures can cause severe network congestion from massive simultaneous data transmissions and introduce a single point of failure that could disrupt entire network functionalities \cite{egi2025}. To address these limitations, recent frameworks explore decentralized orchestration where models operate as autonomous nodes, allowing edge devices equipped with SLMs to make decisions and collaborate autonomously without a central server \cite{egi2025}.

While existing literature establishes the viability of peer-to-peer collaboration, the design space of decentralized orchestration remains comparatively underexplored for adaptive task coordination among resource-constrained language agents. This motivates the investigation of event-driven blackboard architectures and swarm-style topologies in which individual SLMs coordinate through shared state, local confidence signals, and asynchronous task delegation. In such settings, workload distribution can emerge from lightweight coordination rules rather than from a single centralized scheduler, offering a plausible path toward more scalable and resilient localized intelligence.

\section{The Evaluation Gap: Measuring Beyond Accuracy}
Despite rapid advancements in orchestration mechanisms, a significant gap remains in the holistic evaluation of SLM and LLM deployments. Standard automated benchmarks typically prioritize raw accuracy, while giving less emphasis to operational metrics such as inference energy, latency, cost-effectiveness, privacy, and trustworthiness \cite{wang2025collabperf, energytoken2025}.

When operational metrics such as latency, energy consumption, and API cost are reported, they are often not treated as primary optimization targets. However, evaluating an architecture strictly by output quality can obscure its true viability for deployment \cite{energytoken2025}. For example, using Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting on an SLM can incur an energy overhead of up to 150 times that of the baseline model, in some cases making a directly prompted larger LLM more energy-efficient for the same reasoning task \cite{energytoken2025}.

A growing body of evidence suggests that hybrid and collaborative systems should be assessed using multi-dimensional criteria rather than accuracy alone, including throughput, cost-performance trade-offs, cloud-edge privacy, and trustworthiness \cite{wang2025collabperf}. This motivates the adoption of unified evaluation metrics that penalize disproportionate resource consumption and better reflect real-world deployment constraints.
